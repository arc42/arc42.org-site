More than a template: how you work

The template answers where architecture information belongs; the method answers how you work to produce it. arc42 proposes six core activities, with no fixed order and highly interrelated, so the results of any one refine the others in the next increment. Two principles run through all of it: learn constantly, and iterate with continuous feedback. None of these tasks is ever truly finished.

  1. 01

    Clarify requirements

    Development teams need a robust foundation of goals, requirements and constraints to make targeted decisions. Question quality requirements, categorize functional requirements, identify technical risks: clarify the most important quality requirements before anything else.

  2. 02

    Design structures

    Building a system out of smaller parts is one of the fundamental tasks of architecture. arc42 calls these parts building blocks, deliberately technology-neutral, and structures the system along its domain. This produces the Building Block View, the Runtime View, and the interfaces that connect them with each other and the outside world.

  3. 03

    Design crosscutting concepts

    Some decisions cut across the whole system: base technology and infrastructure, frameworks and libraries, recurring patterns, the approach to build, deployment, test and release. Choose the concepts that actually make your quality goals achievable.

  4. 04

    Communicate and document

    Coordinate decisions with the relevant stakeholders and incorporate their feedback. Communicate intensively in person, and put decisions in writing as sparingly as possible: the template supports both extremes, very sparse and very thorough documentation.

  5. 05

    Accompany the implementation

    Work with your team to ensure the code implements the intended structures and concepts, which means actually looking into the source code. It works both ways: find the gold pieces where developers had better ideas, and coach where a concept was misunderstood.

  6. 06

    Analyze and evaluate

    Ask at regular intervals whether your decisions still achieve the desired effect from today's perspective. A methodical look in the rear-view mirror either confirms your decisions or reveals weaknesses and risks early enough to plan improvements.
