Four typical documentation problems hinder software projects. You have probably met them all.

## Not existing — or outdated

![A destroyed old bus in the desert](/images/desert-junk.webp)
[Photo: Pablo Garcia Saldana](https://unsplash.com/photos/I9qoPr82Csg){: .small}

Documentation was created somewhere in the past; rumor says it was correct a long time ago, and the original authors are unknown or gone. Such *historical artifacts* only waste disk space — they provide no value to anybody.

## Chaotic

![A very chaotic desk](/images/chaotic-desk.webp){: .align-left}{: width="40%"}
Documentation created without a clear purpose, by various people, without coordination. Such *documentation anarchy* lacks structure, is hard to maintain, and is generally more harmful than helpful.

[Photo: AuntyLaurie](https://www.flickr.com/photos/auntylaurie/8239863846/){: .small}

## Too much

![A pile of documents stacked on top of each other](/images/document-pile.webp){: .align-right}{: width="30%"}
You can hide any needle in a sufficiently large haystack. Piles of documents may look impressive — until somebody tries to *find* or *update* a specific piece of information. This overflow typically grows on network shares and wikis where nobody cares about structure and content.

[Photo: Alexandre Duret-Lutz](https://www.flickr.com/photos/gadl/320300354/){: .small}

## Painful

![A man screaming in pain](/images/painful.webp){: .align-left}{: width="30%"}
From a developer's perspective, documentation is often plain pain: formalities, inappropriate tools and formats, missing goals, unclear instructions on what to document and how. Stakeholders complain, creativity is gone — that's **not** why people became developers.

[Photo: MattysFlicks](https://www.flickr.com/photos/68397968@N07/9933927445/){: .small}

## How arc42 fixes this

arc42 gives every kind of architecture information a clear place — twelve well-defined sections, optimized for understandability and adequacy. That solves *chaotic* and *too much*. And it solves *painful* through what our users call **painless documentation**: working with arc42 requires hardly any extra effort, because

* you only describe what your stakeholders really need to know,
* you only explain what is necessary to understand the system and its design, and
* you only keep track of the important decisions you had to make anyhow.

Lightweight by intent, arc42 adapts easily to your specific needs — and low-effort documentation is documentation that actually stays up to date, which takes care of *outdated*, too.
